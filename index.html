ABOUT HEPATITIS C

The hepatitis C virus causes a liver infection called hepatitis C. Hepatitis C virus is a leading cause of liver-related deaths, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma. (Millman, 2017) Hepatitis C is transmitted by blood contact with an infected individual. The majority of people today contract hepatitis C by sharing needles or other equipment used to prepare and inject drugs. Hepatitis C is a short-term sickness for some people, but for more than half of those who contract the virus, it becomes a long-term, chronic condition. Cirrhosis and liver cancer are two major, potentially life-threatening side effects of chronic hepatitis C. Chronic hepatitis C patients frequently have no symptoms and do not feel ill. Symptoms of severe liver disease frequently show when they first appear. Hepatitis C does not have a vaccination. Avoiding actions that can transmit the disease, such as injecting drugs, is the greatest method to prevent hepatitis C. It's critical to get tested for hepatitis C because most people with the disease can be cured in 8 to 12 weeks with the right therapy

Chronic hepatitis C is caused by infections with hepatitis C virus (HCV). Due to mild symptoms of patients with chronic hepatitis C, it is difficult to make a clear diagnosis based on clinical manifestations. Using machine learning algorithms to classify and predict the progression of chronic hepatitis C can be targeted for treatment.

Chronic hepatitis C is a blood-borne disease caused by hepatitis C virus infection. The number of people infected with HCV is estimated at ~130 million word-wide (Lara, Khudyakov, Rossi, & Vaughan, 2015).

Virus: Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)

Scientific name: Hepacivirus

Causes:

  • Exposure to blood from unsafe injection practices
  • Unsafe health care
  • Unscreened blood transfusions
  • Injection drug use
  • Sexual practices that lead to exposure to blood

Cure: Currently no effective vaccine against hepatitis C
